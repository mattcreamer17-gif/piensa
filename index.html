Prepared for Piensa Labs

A read on where Piensa Labs sits in the AI-plus-geospatial stack, from someone who thinks you are in the right place at the wrong moment.

Piensa Labs built the Billion Object Platform for Harvard, ships GeoNode code into 24-plus country programs through OpenDRI, and keeps a six-person team of geospatial engineers, ML specialists, and a frontend developer who paints in oil. That is a rare combination. It is also quiet on the public surface right now, which is probably a choice, and probably costing you meetings. This page is a short read on how Eduba would think about the moment you are in.

“Piensa has the technical substrate. Eduba has the orchestration layer. The right move is a partnership.”

The frame

Our working view is that most teams automate the wrong layer. Roughly 60 percent of any given problem is traditional code, about 30 percent is rule-based logic, and the last 10 percent is where real AI earns its cost. Spatial ML work is interesting because it does not fit that split cleanly. GeoNode, PostGIS, and tile caches are the 60 percent. Classical ML on spatial features is the 30 percent. And the new 10 percent, the part where orchestration and agents actually matter, is the part the market is loudest about and most confused by. Piensa already knows the first two layers cold. The open question is how to fold the third in without losing the engineering discipline that defines the first two.

Where we would come in

Eduba designs the orchestration layer that sits on top of existing stacks. Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM) gives a small team a reproducible way to structure agent context, audit decisions, and ship interpretable systems. The two skills are complementary. A recent scoped sprint with Feeld's CTO delivered a workshop, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework to a product company with a technical buyer. That shape of engagement is the kind a Piensa-plus-Eduba pair could co-deliver on a mutual client: Eduba designs the orchestration and context layer, Piensa builds the geospatial and ML substrate.

Pillars that support our actions

Right layer, right tool

Decide which layer each piece of a problem belongs on: AI, traditional code, rule-based logic, human judgment, or "don't build it at all." Most teams pick the loudest layer. That costs money.

Open by default

ICM is MIT-licensed on GitHub and submitted to ACM TiiS. Folder-structured agent architecture, published openly. The register Piensa already publishes in.

Partners, not silos

A small principal shop that holds the methodology, with delivery partners who bring depth in their own verticals. Piensa would fit that pattern on the geospatial side.
